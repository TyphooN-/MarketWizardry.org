Step 1: Understanding VaR Methods

1 Choose Your VaR Method

Our calculator supports three VaR calculation methods, each with specific use cases:

Parametric VaR (Normal Distribution)

  • Best for: Traditional portfolios with normal return distributions
  • Speed: Fastest calculation method
  • Accuracy: Good for stable markets, poor for extreme volatility
  • Formula: VaR = Portfolio Value √ó Volatility √ó Z-score √ó ‚àöTime

Historical VaR (Empirical Distribution)

  • Best for: Cryptocurrency portfolios with non-normal distributions
  • Data Required: 1-3 years of historical returns
  • Accuracy: Reflects actual market behavior and tail risks
  • Limitation: Assumes future volatility matches historical patterns

Monte Carlo VaR (Simulation-Based)

  • Best for: Complex portfolios with multiple asset classes
  • Computational: Most resource-intensive method
  • Flexibility: Can model various distribution assumptions
  • Use Case: Professional risk management and regulatory compliance
Example: For a Bitcoin/Ethereum portfolio, use Historical VaR due to crypto's fat-tailed return distributions. For a traditional 60/40 stock/bond portfolio, Parametric VaR provides sufficient accuracy with faster calculation.

Step 2: Setting Up Your Calculation

2 Portfolio Value Input

Enter your current portfolio market value in USD. This should represent the total value of all positions you want to analyze for risk exposure.

3 Time Horizon Selection

Choose your VaR time horizon based on your trading style and risk management needs:

  • 1-Day VaR: Day traders, active portfolio management, daily risk limits
  • 10-Day VaR: Basel III regulatory compliance, institutional risk management
  • 1-Month VaR: Long-term investors, quarterly portfolio reviews

Example: If you're an active crypto trader, use 1-day VaR for daily position sizing. If you're HODLing, 30-day VaR provides better long-term risk perspective.

  • 95% Confidence: Standard for most trading applications, expect 1 in 20 days to exceed VaR
  • 99% Confidence: Conservative risk management, regulatory compliance, expect 1 in 100 days to exceed VaR
  • 99.9% Confidence: Extreme risk aversion, stress testing, expect 1 in 1000 days to exceed VaR

Step 3: Data Input and Calculation

5 Volatility Data Input

Provide historical data for VaR calculation based on your chosen method:

For Parametric VaR:

  • Annual volatility percentage (e.g., 25% for moderate risk assets)
  • Can use implied volatility from options markets
  • Cryptocurrency typical ranges: 50-150% annual volatility
  • Traditional equity typical ranges: 15-30% annual volatility

For Historical VaR:

  • Upload CSV file with daily return data (minimum 252 trading days)
  • Format: Date, Return (decimal format, e.g., 0.05 for 5% gain)
  • Ensure data quality - remove corporate actions, dividends adjustments
  • Include weekends for cryptocurrency data (24/7 markets)

For Monte Carlo VaR:

  • Historical volatility estimates for distribution parameters
  • Correlation matrix for multi-asset portfolios
  • Number of simulation runs (recommend 10,000+ for accuracy)
  • Distribution assumptions (normal, t-distribution, or empirical)

Step 4: Interpreting Your Results

7 Understanding VaR Output

Your VaR result represents the maximum expected loss over your chosen time horizon at the specified confidence level:

Example Interpretation:
Portfolio Value: $100,000
1-Day VaR at 95% Confidence: $2,500
Meaning: Under normal market conditions, there's a 95% probability that daily losses will not exceed $2,500. Conversely, there's a 5% chance (about 1 day per month) that losses could exceed $2,500.

Key Metrics Provided:

  • Absolute VaR: Maximum loss amount in currency units
  • Relative VaR: Maximum loss as percentage of portfolio value
  • Volatility Adjusted VaR: VaR scaled for current market volatility
  • Confidence Interval: Range of possible VaR estimates

Step 5: Practical Applications

8 Risk Management Applications

Position Sizing

Use VaR to determine appropriate position sizes based on risk tolerance:

  • Daily risk budget: Limit total portfolio VaR to 1-3% of capital
  • Single position limit: Individual position VaR should not exceed 0.5-1% of portfolio
  • Sector concentration: Combined VaR of related positions under 2% of portfolio

Stop Loss Placement

Set stop losses based on VaR multiples:

  • Conservative: Stop loss at 1.5x daily VaR
  • Moderate: Stop loss at 2x daily VaR
  • Aggressive: Stop loss at 3x daily VaR (higher risk tolerance)

Portfolio Rebalancing

Monitor VaR changes to trigger portfolio adjustments:

  • VaR increase >50%: Consider reducing position sizes
  • VaR decrease >30%: Opportunity to increase exposure
  • VaR stability: Maintain current allocation

Mining Operation Risk

10 Backtesting and Validation

Validate your VaR model through backtesting:

  • Exception Rate: Count VaR breaches over time (should match confidence level)
  • Independence Test: VaR breaches should be randomly distributed
  • Kupiec Test: Statistical test for VaR model accuracy
  • Rolling Validation: Continuously monitor model performance
Model Limitations: VaR models assume normal market conditions and may underestimate tail risks during black swan events. Always complement VaR with stress testing and scenario analysis.
